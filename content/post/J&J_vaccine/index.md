First, let’s address a word that you might be hearing about: efficacy. **Efficacy** is a comparison of folks who got COVID symptoms in the placebo group vs. the vaccinated group in a clinical trial over the same time period. Even after information from vaccine trials is released, and even after vaccines receive approval, scientists continue to monitor those individuals from the trials to learn more about where the vaccines shine and how the vaccines can be improved to be more efficacious.

Here is a short summary of the **efficacy** of the Johnson & Johnson vaccine, which was tested in three locations (USA, Latin America, South Africa): 
- 100% protection from death 28 days after a single dose
- 100% protection from severe disease after 49 days **against all variants**
- 85% protection from severe disease after 28 days
- 66% protection from moderate/severe disease after 28 days

<img src= "jj.JPG">

This looks like a great option for vaccination, based on the data. There is room for improvement, and I’m sure J&J will continue investigating what they can do with a booster shot. Another benefit of this vaccine is that it can be stored at refrigerator temperature, which has major implications for getting this vaccine distributed around the world.
[Press release from J&J here](https://www.jnj.com/johnson-johnson-announces-single-shot-janssen-covid-19-vaccine-candidate-met-primary-endpoints-in-interim-analysis-of-its-phase-3-ensemble-trial)

The vaccine is a viral vector vaccine that uses an attenuated (= can’t make you sick) version of a human cold virus to deliver the info for the SARS-CoV-2 spike to your immune system. It is most similar to the [AstraZeneca vaccine.](https://dradrian.netlify.app/post/astrazeneca/)
